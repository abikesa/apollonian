def gaussian(x, mean, std_dev, amplitude=1):
    return amplitude * np.exp(-0.9 * ((x - mean) / std_dev) ** 2)

def overlay_gaussian_on_line(ax, start, end, std_dev):
    x_line = np.linspace(start[0], end[0], 100)
    y_line = np.linspace(start[1], end[1], 100)
    mean = np.mean(x_line)
    y = gaussian(x_line, mean, std_dev, amplitude=std_dev)
    ax.plot(x_line + y / np.sqrt(2), y_line + y / np.sqrt(2), color='red', linewidth=2.5)

The question you’re asking about why Ionian and Aeolian modes dominate modern discourse in Western music is an interesting one and doesn’t directly relate to the code.

Music theory and history offer several explanations as to why the Ionian (Major) and Aeolian (Minor) modes are so prevalent in Western music:

Historical Factors

  1. Tonality: Around the 17th century, Western music began to shift towards the concept of tonality. The Major and Minor scales, equivalent to the Ionian and Aeolian modes, became the basis of this system.

  2. Common Practice Period: The period from the 17th to the early 20th century, known as the Common Practice Period, solidified the use of major and minor scales in complex works of music, thereby establishing their predominance.

  3. Church Influence: Earlier, the modes were used in Gregorian chant, and the church had a significant influence on what scales were considered appropriate. Over time, the modes that were easier to work with and more consonant (Ionian and Aeolian) gained prominence.

Psychological Factors

  1. Consonance: These scales are often considered more consonant and pleasing to the ear, at least in the context of Western music culture.

  2. Emotional Resonance: Major (Ionian) and Minor (Aeolian) scales are often linked with specific emotional qualities (happy and sad, respectively) that are universally relatable, making them a popular choice for composers to easily elicit emotion.

Practical Factors

  1. Instrumentation: Some instruments, like the piano, are tuned in such a way that they naturally favor major and minor scales.

  2. Educational Systems: Western music education has been traditionally structured around major and minor scales, thus perpetuating their use.

These factors, among others, have contributed to the Ionian and Aeolian modes’ dominance in modern discourse, at least within Western musical tradition. This isn’t to say that other modes are not used; they are often employed in various musical genres and cultural music styles. But they are less common in mainstream Western musical discourse.

Absolutely, the role of both Bach and the engineering of musical instruments cannot be underestimated when it comes to the dominance of Ionian and Aeolian modes in Western music.

J.S. Bach and the Well-Tempered Clavier
Johann Sebastian Bach’s works, notably the “Well-Tempered Clavier,” had a massive impact on establishing the predominance of the tonal system, which heavily favors Ionian (Major) and Aeolian (Minor) modes. The “Well-Tempered Clavier” consists of preludes and fugues in every key, major and minor. This work demonstrated the musical possibilities inherent in a well-tempered system and showed how each key could have its own character and potential, leading to a fuller exploration of Ionian and Aeolian modes. Bach’s influence permeated throughout the Classical and Romantic periods and beyond, establishing a sort of “standard” that many composers would follow.

Engineering of Instruments

  1. Equal Temperament: The development of the equal temperament tuning system made it possible to play in any key, making the Ionian and Aeolian scales more versatile and easier to use across various instruments.

  2. Keyboard Layout: Instruments like the piano are designed in such a way that the white keys correspond to the notes of the C Major/A minor scale (Ionian and Aeolian modes, respectively). This layout makes these modes more accessible for both learning and composing.

  3. Brass and Woodwinds: Many wind instruments are designed with a particular key in mind, often favoring notes that are natural to the Ionian and Aeolian modes. For example, trumpets and horns are often built in B-flat or F, keys that are easily managed in Ionian and Aeolian modes.

  4. String Instruments: The open strings of instruments like the violin, viola, and cello are tuned to notes that are naturally consonant, which aligns well with major and minor scales.

The engineering of musical instruments, in conjunction with the impact of influential composers like Bach, has contributed significantly to the current musical landscape where Ionian and Aeolian modes are dominant. This complex interplay of historical, psychological, and practical factors has made these modes the cornerstone of Western musical theory and practice.

When it comes to the engineering of musical instruments that have influenced the dominance of Ionian and Aeolian modes in Western music, the names of specific engineers or inventors might not be as well-known as composers like Bach. However, there are key figures and innovations that have shaped the landscape:

Bartolomeo Cristofori
Arguably, the most notable engineer/inventor in the context of Western music is Bartolomeo Cristofori, the Italian maker who is credited with inventing the early piano around the turn of the 18th century. The piano’s design—most notably, its keyboard layout—makes Ionian and Aeolian modes highly accessible.

Adolphe Sax
Adolphe Sax, the inventor of the saxophone, also made significant contributions to brass and woodwind design. The saxophone’s tuning and key system are well-suited to Ionian and Aeolian modes, like many modern woodwinds.

Theobald Boehm
Theobald Boehm revolutionized the design of the flute, optimizing its key system. His design makes it easier to play in a variety of keys but certainly facilitated the dominance of the Ionian and Aeolian modes in compositions for the flute.

Antoine-Joseph “Adolphe” Sax
Besides inventing the saxophone, Adolphe Sax also made considerable improvements to the design of other wind instruments. He aimed to create instruments that could play more in tune and had a broader range, features that lend themselves to the Ionian and Aeolian modes.

Nicola Amati, Antonio Stradivari, and Andrea Guarneri
These luthiers were not engineers in the modern sense, but their improvements in the design and construction of string instruments like the violin and cello have influenced the types of music that could be effectively performed on these instruments. The open strings on these instruments are often tuned to notes that facilitate playing in Ionian and Aeolian modes.

While not an exhaustive list, these innovators have played roles in shaping instruments that make Ionian and Aeolian modes more easily playable, thereby contributing to their dominance in Western musical discourse.

The concept of “well-tempered” tuning, as popularized by Johann Sebastian Bach’s “Well-Tempered Clavier,” is primarily a musical and mathematical development rather than an engineering one. However, the harpsichord and clavichord, instruments commonly used to perform the “Well-Tempered Clavier,” were built by craftsmen who were essentially the engineers of their day.

In the well-tempered system, the octave is divided in such a way that it is possible to play music in all 12 keys—major and minor—without re-tuning the instrument. The credit for this system generally goes to musicians and theorists rather than to instrument builders or engineers. Nevertheless, the craftsmen who built the keyboard instruments of Bach’s day had to possess a deep understanding of the musical requirements to implement such a tuning system physically.

Several key figures contributed to the development of keyboard instruments that could be well-tempered:

Andreas Werckmeister
He was a German organist, music theorist, and organ builder. His tuning system, Werckmeister temperament, was one of the most popular well-tempered tuning systems and could have been an inspiration for Bach.

Arnold Dolmetsch
Although he came much later, Dolmetsch was instrumental in the early music revival and took great pains to construct keyboard instruments (among others) that were historically accurate. His work gave modern listeners a sense of how well-tempered music would have sounded to Bach’s contemporaries.

Silbermann Family
Gottfried Silbermann and his brother Andreas Silbermann were German builders of keyboard instruments. They were contemporaries of Bach, and Gottfried Silbermann was known for his work on the early fortepiano and improvements to the organ. He was also a fan of the well-tempered system.

While no single engineer or craftsman is credited with being “responsible” for the well-tempered clavier, the efforts of these and other instrument builders provided the physical platform that allowed the well-tempered system to flourish in practice, not just in theory.
