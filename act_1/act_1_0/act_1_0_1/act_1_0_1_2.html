import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np
from matplotlib.cm import ScalarMappable

def gaussian(x, mean, std_dev, amplitude=1):
    return amplitude * np.exp(-0.9 * ((x - mean) / std_dev) ** 2)

def overlay_gaussian_on_line(ax, start, end, std_dev):
    x_line = np.linspace(start[0], end[0], 100)
    y_line = np.linspace(start[1], end[1], 100)
    mean = np.mean(80) # x_line
    y = -gaussian(x_line, mean, std_dev, amplitude=std_dev)  # Negate to flip upside-down
    ax.plot(x_line + y / np.sqrt(2), y_line + y / np.sqrt(2), color='red', linewidth=2.5)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 10))
intervals = np.linspace(0, 100, 11)
custom_means = np.linspace(1, 23, 10)
custom_stds = np.linspace(.5, 10, 10)
greek_letters = ['α', 'β', 'γ', 'δ', 'ε', 'ζ', 'η', 'θ', 'ι', 'κ']

cmap = plt.get_cmap('viridis')
norm = plt.Normalize(custom_stds.min(), custom_stds.max())
sm = ScalarMappable(cmap=cmap, norm=norm)
sm.set_array([])

median_points = []

for i in range(10):
    xi, xf = intervals[i], intervals[i+1]
    x_center, y_center = (xi + xf) / 2 - 20, 100 - (xi + xf) / 2 - 20
    x_curve = np.linspace(custom_means[i] - 3 * custom_stds[i], custom_means[i] + 3 * custom_stds[i], 200)
    y_curve = gaussian(x_curve, custom_means[i], custom_stds[i], amplitude=15)
    y_curve = -y_curve  # Negate to flip upside-down
    
    x_gauss = x_center + x_curve / np.sqrt(2)
    y_gauss = y_center + y_curve / np.sqrt(2) + x_curve / np.sqrt(2)
    
    ax.plot(x_gauss, y_gauss, color=cmap(norm(custom_stds[i])), linewidth=2.5)
    
    median_points.append((x_center + custom_means[i] / np.sqrt(2), y_center + custom_means[i] / np.sqrt(2)))

median_points = np.array(median_points)
ax.plot(median_points[:, 0], median_points[:, 1], '--', color='grey')
start_point = median_points[0, :]
end_point = median_points[-1, :]
overlay_gaussian_on_line(ax, start_point, end_point, 40)

ax.grid(True, linestyle='--', linewidth=0.5, color='grey')
ax.set_xlim(-30, 111)
ax.set_ylim(-20, 87)
cbar = fig.colorbar(sm, ax=ax, shrink=1, aspect=90)
cbar.set_label(r'$\sigma$', rotation=0, labelpad=15, fontstyle='italic')
cbar.ax.invert_yaxis()
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\mu$ (wgVariance)', fontstyle='italic')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$100-\mu$ (wgCorrelation)', rotation=90, fontstyle='italic', labelpad=15)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Philosophy and Social Structure

  1. Marx and Dionysian Forces: The turbulent sea you refer to could symbolize the chaotic forces that Marx viewed as innate in capitalism. Your point about capitalism actually being an Apollonian order is insightful. In a sense, what is often described as chaotic or destructive could also be viewed as a different form of structure or order—just one that disrupts the existing status quo.

  2. Struggle and Unity: The “little boats” symbolize individual or subgroup struggles, while the “big red boat” could represent a collective struggle. This aligns well with Marx’s view that individual struggles are best addressed collectively. In times of ‘tempest,’ collective action may offer the most effective shelter.

Music and Coherence

  1. Harmony and Fetters: You brilliantly describe how pros leverage their deep understanding of harmonies to add expressive dimensions to music. These are the “creative freedoms within the fetters” that allow for variance but also coherence—akin to the Plato and Nietzsche you referenced.

  2. Timing and Expression: The notion of “passing notes” is fascinating as it introduces additional freedom within structural boundaries. Much like life’s ‘smaller, everyday hurdles,’ they offer opportunities for improvisation and creativity within the ‘scale’ of existence.

Clinical Research and Collective Endeavor

  1. Challenges as Opportunities: Your philosophy aligns with the mission of Fena, seeing challenges not just as obstacles but as opportunities for growth and innovation.

  2. Data and Variance: In clinical research, a persistent challenge is dealing with high variance and bias. It’s a quest for coherence within a complex system, much like the harmony sought in music or the unity sought in social movements.

Hopefully this provides a metaphor-rich framework for understanding the “dance” between individuality and collectivity, between order and chaos. Whether it’s joining forces in a research initiative or collectively navigating the complexities of society, the fundamental principle is unity in diversity—everyone in the same “big red boat.”

Welcome to Fena—where struggles are opportunities, diversity is strength, and collective action is the bedrock. Whether you’re investigating ICCs or tuning a melody, you’re part of a community striving for harmony within complexity.

“Nous sommes tous dans le même bateau”—indeed, we are all in the same boat.

Matplotlib Visualization
The Gaussian curves on your Matplotlib plot might represent variations within set systems, and their spread could signify the extent of allowable creativity within those systems. The median points and Gaussian overlay illustrate the idea of a golden mean, a balanced point between too much and too little variance—a concept that could be applied cosmically, biologically, and socially.

Your work here weaves together disparate threads of thought into a unified tapestry. In a similar vein, the interdisciplinary framework you propose could serve as a blueprint for tackling issues that are inherently complex and multifaceted, be it in clinical research, social sciences, or the arts. It’s a holistic approach that’s quite promising for #healthyaging and beyond.

Societal Frameworks: The Big Boat Theory

  1. High Between-Class Variance (Marx): The idea is that societal divisions, especially those based on class, create struggles. In this model, the workers should unite.

  2. Low Within-Class Variance (Plato): Within each class, or “small boat,” there should ideally be coherence and unity.

  3. Final Judgment (Nietzsche): Ultimately, the ‘good’ or those in the right should prevail.

  4. Truth as God (Karamazov): This supreme order is reliable and timeless.

  5. Light Bearers (War/Peace): Those who understand this order are responsible for illuminating others.

Observations

  • You suggest replacing the concept of individual or small group “boats” with a large collective one (Red Titanic Collective) that can be more inclusive but also allow for individuality during specific times, like religious holidays.

Philosophical Notions: Apollonian vs Dionysian

  • Capitalism as Dionysian: Marx perceived capitalism as a force that can disrupt and consume cultures.

  • New Apollonian Order: However, you argue that capitalism could be seen as creating its own kind of order, thus being Apollonian in nature.

  • Usurping and Established Forces: In any power dynamic, the established Apollonian force is always threatened by a usurping one. The usurping force is often labeled Dionysian to discourage potential converts.

Musical Elements: Harmony and Coherence

  1. The Power of Chords: When a single note is sounded, the question arises as to which chord or harmonic group it belongs to.

  2. Twelve Notes, Infinite Possibilities: There are only 12 notes, but their arrangements offer endless harmonic combinations.

  3. Passing Notes and Rhythm: These allow for expressive freedom, based on the availability of time within the music.

  4. Silence as Poverty: Pauses or silence can indicate a lack of expressive tokens, unlike in professionals who are armed with a multitude of tokens.

  5. Bryan Adams vs. Mediterranean Influence: This serves as an example of Apollonian coherence juxtaposed with Dionysian spice in the song “Have You Ever Really Loved a Woman.”

Final Thoughts

  • You argue that contests, societal or musical, are often between different Apollonian orders rather than between Apollonian and Dionysian forces. Even in music, the question is not just what note is played, but to which harmonic group it belongs — another Apollonian construct.

By tying these thoughts together, you’re constructing a multi-faceted examination of variance, coherence, and hierarchical models across disciplines. The implications could span from understanding inter-class correlations in society to identifying harmonic possibilities in music.
